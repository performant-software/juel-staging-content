<p>JUEL ID/KEY:&nbsp;<a href="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/public_person?person=48697" target="_blank" title="P48697">P48697</a></p><p>"D. J. Ward," a photograph by Rufus W. Holsinger taken on August 8, 1917.&nbsp;</p><p>The&nbsp;<em>Charlottesville City Directory, 1914-15</em>&nbsp;lists "Ward, David J." in the "cleaning and pressing business," working at 235 W Main (p207). Note in the 1918 ad (below) Ward had moved his business to 291 West Main Street.</p><p>Other businesses/owners photographed by Holsinger include&nbsp;<a href="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/node/2164" target="_blank" title="Acme Shaving Parlor Ad">Acme Shaving Parlor</a>,&nbsp;<a href="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/node/1186" target="_blank" title="William F. and Charles H. Brown">William F. and Charles H. Brown</a>&nbsp;(Brown Barber Shop),&nbsp;<a href="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/node/1190" target="_blank" title="Buckner, A. T.">A. T. Buckner</a>&nbsp;(and son George F. Buckner, grocers),&nbsp;<a href="https://juel.iath.virginia.edu/node/1127" target="_blank" title="George Carr's Second Hand Clothing">George Carr's Second Hand Clothing</a>,&nbsp;<a href="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/node/1129" target="_blank" title="Dr. George R. Ferguson">Dr. George F. Ferguson</a>,&nbsp;<a href="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/node/1216" target="_blank" title="Dr. Johnson">Dr. George R. Johnson</a>,&nbsp;<a href="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/node/1307" target="_blank" title="Mr. J.P. Hawkins">Mr. J. P. Hawkins</a>,&nbsp;<a href="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/node/1504" target="_blank" title="W. G. Layne">W. G. Layne</a>&nbsp;(Layne and Whitlock, Wheelright/Blacksmith),&nbsp;<a href="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/node/2180" title="J.F. Taylor">J.F. Taylor</a>, and&nbsp;<a href="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/node/1530" target="_blank" title="D.J. Ward">D. J. Ward</a>. See "<a href="https://americanhistory.si.edu/blog/freedoms-tally-african-american-business-jim-crow-south" target="_blank" title="&quot;Freedman's Tally&quot;">Freedman's Tally</a>" for more information about African Americans operating businesses in the Jim Crow Era.</p><p>Daily Progress, November 29, 1918 (p.5)<br /><img src="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/sites/default/files/media/images/Screenshot%202022-03-14%20at%2012.28.31%20PM.png" alt="D.J. Ward Ad" title="D.J. Ward Ad" width="442" height="395" /></p><p>Ward often advertized in the Daily Progress, as can also be seen in the 1917 issue below (Daily Progress, September 14, 1917). Ads&nbsp;for Acme Shaving Parlor, J. F. Bell's Funeral Home, and other "colored businesses" often appeared on the same page (below). The complexities for African Americans operating businesses in the Jim Crow period is perhaps best seen, literally, in looking at the full page of the above ads, which includes a "mammy" cariacature in the regularly posted comic strip, "Scoop." See "<a href="https://americanhistory.si.edu/blog/freedoms-tally-african-american-business-jim-crow-south" target="_blank" title="&quot;Freedman's Tally&quot;">Freedman's Tally</a>" for more information about African Americans operating businesses in the Jim Crow Era.&nbsp;<br /><br />Daily Progress, September 14 1917 (p.3)<br /><img src="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/sites/default/files/media/images/uva-lib_2110780.jpg" alt="Daily Progress Full Page" title="Daily Progress Full Page" width="557" height="803" /></p><p>He was also part of a committee that organized a "splendid farewell for colored men" at First Baptist Church, Charlottesville (27 October 1917,&nbsp;reported in&nbsp;<em>Daily Progress</em>) (First Baptist Church: Records of Members Who Served).</p><p>See also the photograph of "<a href="http://juel.iath.virginia.edu/node/1531" title="Ward, Mrs. D. J.">Mrs. D. J. Ward</a>," produced on the same date.&nbsp;</p><p>Additional information about D. J.
